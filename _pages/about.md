Hi, it's Zafer [/zɑ·fɛɾˈ/](http://ipa-reader.com/?text=z%C9%91%C2%B7f%C9%9B%C9%BE%E2%80%99&voice=Filiz).

I’m a cybersecurity, compliance and IT operations professional based in Tallinn, Estonia. My work sits at the point where security governance, infrastructure, defensive engineering and operational resilience meet. I have worked across software development, systems administration, infrastructure, IT management and cybersecurity, with experience in financial-sector security and compliance as well as earlier defense and NATO environments.

My focus is practical security: how controls behave in real environments, how systems fail, and how organizations can make security decisions that remain usable under operational pressure. I am less interested in security as a collection of slogans and more interested in whether the detection works, whether the evidence survives scrutiny, whether the recovery plan can be executed, and whether the control still makes sense once it reaches production.

That work has shaped how I think about cybersecurity. Governance matters, but only if it connects to implementation. Detection engineering matters, but only if detections can be tested, tuned and maintained. Compliance matters, but only if it improves accountability instead of becoming paperwork around unresolved risk. Infrastructure matters because most security outcomes depend on systems that must keep running while they are being protected.

I founded [DeltaZulu OÜ](https://deltazulu.ee) with my partner in life and work as a small Tallinn-based technology company focused on cybersecurity, infrastructure and defensive engineering. Through it, I publish selected open-source tools, experiments and technical work around security operations, DNS, telemetry, detection, log analysis and infrastructure security. Some of that work is exploratory. Some of it comes from operational friction. The common thread is the same: turn practical problems into tools, explanations or repeatable methods that others can inspect and use.

This site is where I write about cybersecurity from that implementation angle. You will find articles about defensive engineering, SIEM design, detection-as-code, Wazuh, DNS security, Windows administration, log analysis, operational resilience, compliance, and the human side of security. I write for practitioners who need security ideas to survive contact with real systems, real people and real constraints.

I also care about teaching. Security knowledge has limited value if it cannot be explained clearly enough for others to apply it. Whether I am writing, mentoring, building small tools or preparing training material, I try to make complex security topics more usable without flattening their difficulty.

## What You’ll Find Here

This site is built for people who want security analysis with implementation detail, not recycled vendor language.

**Defensive engineering.** Practical work on detections, logs, telemetry, infrastructure and the systems that support security operations.

**Security operations.** SIEM design, alert quality, detection content, evidence handling, incident candidates, threat hunting and operational workflows.

**Governance, risk and compliance.** Security management, control design, auditability, business continuity, disaster recovery and the gap between written controls and working controls.

**Infrastructure security.** DNS, identity, Windows administration, Linux systems, network services and the operational foundations that security teams depend on.

**Open-source security tooling.** Experiments, integrations and utilities built to solve specific defensive or operational problems.

**Human and organizational aspects of security.** Training, behaviour, decision-making, incentives, tabletop exercises and the limits of awareness alone.

No padded thought leadership. No vendor theatre. No claim that a tool solves a problem just because a dashboard says it does. The goal is to examine how security actually works, where it breaks, and what can be improved without pretending the environment is cleaner than it is.

## Default to Defensible

Security should not be theatre performed after an incident, an audit or a failed control review. It should be built into the way systems are operated, monitored, tested and improved.

That does not mean every organization needs more tools, more dashboards or more noise. It means security work should leave behind something defensible: clearer evidence, better detections, stronger recovery assumptions, more reliable controls, and decisions that can be explained when conditions are no longer ideal.

If you are a defender, engineer, security leader, auditor, consultant or practitioner trying to make security more operationally real, this site is for you.
