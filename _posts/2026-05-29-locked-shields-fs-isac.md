On 21 May, I presented to FS-ISAC members on why [Exercise Locked Shields](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SiqHAuJGXaY) matters for financial institutions. The official teaser gives a public sense of the scale and atmosphere, but my focus was narrower. I wanted to discuss what happens when a cyber incident stops being only a systems problem and becomes a sector coordination problem.

{% include gallery id="galleryLS26" caption="Collaboration is our protection" %}

One phrase from the exercise stayed with me: collaboration is our protection. It is a simple sentence, and therefore easy to treat as a slogan. I do not think it should be read that way. For the financial sector, collaboration is a resilience constraint. No single bank, payment provider, supervisor, CERT, ministry, or sector body can manage a national-scale financial cyber crisis alone. The response depends on relationships, decision rights, information flows, legal thresholds, communications discipline, and operational fallbacks that need to exist before the crisis begins.

That was the point I tried to make in the session. Financial institutions already spend significant effort on security, continuity, crisis management, third-party risk, and regulatory obligations. These disciplines are often managed through separate structures, with separate plans and separate vocabulary. A serious incident does not respect those boundaries. Availability, integrity, public confidence, supervisory expectations, legal constraints, and communications pressure can arrive together, before the facts are complete and before every stakeholder has the same operating picture.

Locked Shields is useful because it creates a controlled environment where those pressures can be tested together. It is often described as a large cyber-defence exercise, which is accurate but incomplete. The technical layer matters, but it is not the whole exercise. Systems are attacked, defended, restored, and analysed. At the same time, teams must make decisions under uncertainty, communicate with constrained information, assess legal and strategic implications, and coordinate across public and private actors.

For the financial sector, that combination is the point.

A cyber incident affecting a financial service provider is not automatically a systemic crisis. Scale alone is not enough. The threshold changes when concentration, limited substitutability, interconnected dependencies, uncertainty about transaction integrity, and public confidence start interacting. A disruption can begin as a technical or vendor issue, but its consequences may quickly move into business continuity, market confidence, public services, and national-level crisis management.

This is why financial-sector participation in exercises like Locked Shields should not be treated as symbolic. Sectors create realism. Banks, payment providers, supervisors, central banks, infrastructure operators, legal advisers, communications specialists, business-continuity teams, and crisis managers each see different parts of the same incident. Without that mixture, a financial scenario may be technically plausible but operationally thin. With it, the exercise can expose where sector assumptions are strong, where they are merely documented, and where they have not yet been tested under pressure.

This year, I supported the STRATEX track as a gray team member for financial-sector incidents. My role included developing financial-crisis material and related questions, and I also acted as a liaison point for FS-ISAC members through an online forum. That combination gave me two perspectives on the same problem. Scenario work forces structural thinking: which dependency matters, how fast consequences become visible, who must decide, and what information is still missing. Liaison work shows the practical side: coordination depends on channels, timing, shared vocabulary, and enough trust to act before the picture is complete.

There was also a very ordinary operational side to it. At times, I was moving between scenario work, liaison activity, exercise coordination, and the practical reality of keeping different channels open at the same time. Managing two laptops is not a profound lesson in resilience, but it is a familiar reminder that coordination work is rarely clean. Even in an exercise, the quality of the outcome depends on tools, timing, context switching, imperfect handoffs, and people trying to keep a shared picture alive.

The lesson is not that every institution needs to participate in the same way. Some organizations may contribute technical specialists through national teams. Others may provide crisis-management, legal, communications, business-continuity, or sector-specific expertise. Some may observe first, support scenario development, participate through rehearsal events, or coordinate through sector bodies. The useful question is not simply whether an organization can join. It is what assumption the organization wants to test.

For a bank, that may be where internal continuity ends and ecosystem resilience begins. For a payment provider, it may be whether recovery priorities are understood outside the technical team. For a supervisor or central bank, it may be whether escalation, reporting, concentration risk, and public-private coordination are visible early enough. For a sector body, it may be whether members can move from parallel institutional responses to a shared operating picture.

This is also where exercises complement regulatory work. DORA and NIS2 set expectations around resilience, incident management, third-party risk, and coordination. Locked Shields is not a regulatory audit, and it should not be reduced to one. Its value is different. It places similar problems under operational stress: shared-provider failure, incomplete information, public messaging, escalation, legal pressure, technical recovery, and coordination across institutions and authorities.

Plans are necessary, but they are not the same as practiced coordination. A business-continuity plan can describe fallback arrangements. An exercise can show whether those arrangements scale, who authorizes them, how they are communicated, and what happens when several organizations need the same alternative at the same time. An incident-response plan can define technical recovery. An exercise can test whether recovery is trusted when data integrity, customer confidence, and public messaging are still contested.

That mismatch is where the exercise becomes useful.

Locked Shields makes the boundary between institutional resilience and sector resilience visible. It shows where a problem remains internal, where it becomes shared, and where it becomes impossible to manage without prior coordination. For financial institutions, that is the real value. The exercise is not only about defending systems. It is about discovering whether the sector can still make coherent decisions when systems, confidence, and time are all under pressure.

I was glad to present this perspective to FS-ISAC members, partly because the topic sits close to how I think about resilience in practice. The interesting part is rarely the existence of a plan. It is whether the plan survives contact with dependencies, incomplete information, institutional boundaries, and public pressure. Exercises like Locked Shields give us a way to test that without waiting for a real crisis to do it for us.

The strongest future participation will not come from treating Locked Shields as an annual cyber event. It will come from treating it as a resilience rehearsal: a place to test decision-making, dependencies, escalation, communications, and public-private coordination before a real incident removes the margin for improvisation.

The sector does not need more abstract agreement that resilience matters. It needs more practiced coordination around the conditions under which resilience is actually tested.
